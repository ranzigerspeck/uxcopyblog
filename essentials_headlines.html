UX Writing Essentials: Headlines

UX Writing Essentials: Headlines

Key points – Headlines summarise important information and encourage users to take action. Keep your headlines simple, concise, address your users directly and give them a reason to proceed.

18 May 2020 · 
Link copied!

As a UX writer, you’ll encounter many different kinds of headlines. There are headlines on top of blog or news articles, headings in pop-up messages, payment or onboarding flows, email subject lines or push notifications.

Just to get the semantics out of the way: you wouldn’t necessarily call all of those “headlines”. But they all serve a similar purpose and follow similar mechanics: if you’re summarising important information and you want the user to act on it, you’re dealing with a headline. So let’s use “headlines” as an umbrella term and find out how they work.

Also, this is not a step-by-step guide for writing the perfect headline. It’s a collection of insights and techniques I find useful when writing headlines. I usually start with these two high-level thoughts:

  • Get to the point
  • Make it engaging

Let’s explore what that means and how it works.

Get to the point

Great headlines condense important information into very few words. Have a look at these historical newspaper headlines:

  • “Men Walk on Moon” (The New York Times, July 21, 1969)
  • “Ford to City: Drop Dead” (New York Daily News, October 30, 1975)
  • “Nixon Resigns” (Washington Post, August 9, 1974)

Why are these headlines so effective? Obviously, they describe major events that shaped the course of history. But they're also very successful at the following:

They’re short
Like, really short. The headlines above condense major events into the smallest amount of words possible. The third headline (“Nixon resigns”) literally consists of just two words.

They’re simple
They're simple sentences using simple words: Even if you just learned to read, you’ll be able to understand “Men walk on moon”. On average, it takes a second-grade reading level to understand all three headlines.

They clarify
These headlines convey big stories with great clarity. Even if you’re hurrying past the newsstand (let’s imagine it’s 1975 for a second), briefly glancing at the newspapers, you’ll know exactly what has happened.

They summarise
Good headlines summarise complex content effectively. Have a look at the second headline example: In 1975, US President Gerald Ford gave a speech denying federal assistance to spare New York City from bankruptcy. He didn’t actually say “Drop dead” – but it’s a very effective way of summing up what his speech meant to the city, at least according to the New York Daily News.

Let's put this 👆 into practice
OK, say you’re writing a headline for a new photo-sharing app called “PhotoSnapper” (hey, I’m not a naming expert). Start by writing down what you want to say. Don’t think about the length, grammar or style at this point. Just say what you want to say.

Then, start editing. Aim to make your headline as snappy and engaging as possible. Let’s say you’re starting with this:

PhotoSnapper is a new app that lets you snap, upload and share your photos with friends, family and people around the world.

When writing about a product, don't just focus on the product itself and how great it is. Instead, show what a user can do with it. This puts the user in the centre of your communication and gives them a feeling of being in control.

PhotoSnapper is a new app that lets you snap, upload and share your photos with friends, family and people around the world.

Also, highlight the major benefit of your product. You can do this by stripping out any secondary or less important features from your copy.

PhotoSnapper is a new app that lets you snap, upload and share your photos with friends, family and people around the world.

Time to summarise: just like you don’t have to mention every single feature in your headline, you can just go for the biggest user group.

PhotoSnapper is a new app that lets you snap, upload and share your photos with friends, family and people around the world.

Also note how we’re using very simple language and addressing our users directly (more on that in a minute). Here’s our new, shorter and punchier headline:

PhotoSnapper. Share your photos with the world.

Make it engaging

We’ve discussed how to write concise and clear headlines. But in UX writing, headlines also have to be actionable. They don’t just describe something, they encourage the user to take a specific action. Here's a simple example – asking the user to sign up to a newsletter:

Talk directly to the user
Imagine you’re having a conversation with your users. A simple way to achieve that is by addressing users directly and by leading with a verb. In our newsletter headline example, “Subscribe” serves this purpose.

Also, in the spirit of having a conversation, don’t refer to yourself or your organisation in the third person. Instead of “Subscribe to PhotoSnapper’s newsletter”, write “Subscribe to our newsletter”.

Headlines and CTAs
In many cases, headlines will be paired with CTAs. Of course there’s usually other content between the two, but when writing copy, pay attention to the relation between a headline and the CTA. Headlines and user flows

In UX writing, a headline is often part of a larger flow. While the example above is a single pop-up, you’re often more likely to write headlines for sign-up, onboarding or payment flows.

That means you can spread the information in your headlines across multiple screens. Ask yourself: what does the user need to know at each point of the flow? Let’s have a look at an example. We’re designing an onboarding flow for our imaginary app “PhotoSnapper”. On a high level, the structure looks like this:

The “Introduction” headline could just be the one we’ve already written: “PhotoSnapper. Share your photos with the world.” – a short punchy summary of what the user can do with the app.

In “Feature highlights”, we can go into a bit more detail. For example, we could say, “Take brilliant photos” on the first screen and “Easily share photos on your favourite social networks” on the second. Not the most exciting features, I know, but we’re just talking about the structure of the flow here: get users interested with your first headlines, explain your product’s top features on the following screens, and, in the “Sign up” section, give them a reason to take action.

This reason can be all kinds of things, whether it’s something that’s intangible (“Join over 1m inspiring creators”) or something that has actual monetary value (“30-day free trial”), make sure they fit your product story and resonate with your audience.

There are no shortcuts

On a final note, there are no shortcuts to a great headline. You’ll probably write dozens of them before you find one or two you’re happy with. Look at your product from different angles, try different stylistic devices, discuss with your team, test your headlines. It takes a lot of work to make a headline sound fun and easy-going.
